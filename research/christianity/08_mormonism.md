# Mormonism (Latter-day Saints) — Origins, Evidence, and Analysis

## Overview

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS Church) is the largest
denomination within the broader Latter-day Saint movement. Founded by Joseph
Smith in 1830 in upstate New York, it now claims ~17.5 million members worldwide
(as of Dec 2024). It is one of the most successful new religious movements in
modern history — and one of the most scrutinised.

This document applies the same historical-critical method we used for
Christianity: what does the evidence show, what is claimed, and where do those
two diverge?

---

## Executive Summary — Why This Case Is So Revealing

Mormonism's foundational claims are **uniquely testable** because they are
modern (< 200 years old) — and they **fail every test**:

- **Book of Abraham**: The original Egyptian papyri were rediscovered in 1966.
  Every qualified Egyptologist confirms they are standard funerary texts.
  Smith's "translation" is completely wrong.
- **Book of Mormon archaeology**: Zero supporting evidence. No city, no
  battlefield, no writing system, no artefact. The Smithsonian Institution and
  National Geographic Society have formally denied any connection.
- **DNA**: Indigenous Americans descend from East Asia, not Israel. The church's
  own essays concede the traditional ancestry claim is unsupported.
- **Anachronisms**: Horses, steel, wheat, chariots, elephants — none existed in
  pre-Columbian Americas, yet feature prominently in the Book of Mormon.
- **Kinderhook Plates**: Smith attempted to "translate" a known 19th-century
  hoax — and the LDS Church's own *History of the Church* records it.
- **The LDS Church itself now admits** (in its 2013–2014 Gospel Topics Essays)
  the multiple contradictory First Vision accounts, the seer stone in a hat,
  the Book of Abraham papyrus problem, the DNA problem, and Smith's secret
  polygamy with teenagers and other men's wives.

**Why, then, do 17.5 million people believe it?** For the same reasons people
believe any religion — and this is the most revealing part:

1. **Childhood indoctrination** — most members were raised in the faith and
   baptised at 8, long before critical thinking develops.
2. **Community and identity** — the LDS Church provides an extraordinarily
   tight-knit social world. Leaving means losing your community, sometimes
   your marriage, often your family relationships.
3. **Emotional epistemology** — Mormonism teaches that truth is confirmed by
   feelings ("pray about it; if you feel warm, it's true"). This is
   indistinguishable from the confirmation bias every human brain is subject
   to. Hindus, Muslims, and Evangelicals all report identical spiritual
   confirmations of *their* mutually exclusive beliefs.
4. **Sunk cost** — 10% of lifetime income (tithing), 2 years of youth
   (mission), decades of service. Admitting it was wrong means confronting
   an enormous personal loss.
5. **Information control** — until the internet, the church successfully kept
   its own history from members. The CES Letter (2013) and online access to
   primary sources have shattered that control, triggering a generational
   exodus: Gen Z and Millennial Latter-day Saints are now leaving at record
   rates (confirmed by *Religion News Service*, Dec 2025; General Social
   Survey data, 1973–2022).

**The broader lesson**: Mormonism is not an anomaly. It is a case study in how
*all* religions operate. The difference is only that Mormonism is young enough
to have a complete historical record. The same mechanisms — childhood
socialisation, community pressure, emotional confirmation, sunk cost, and
information asymmetry — sustain every religion on Earth. Mormonism simply makes
the machinery visible because we can fact-check its claims with modern tools
(archaeology, genetics, Egyptology) in ways we cannot for religions founded in
antiquity.

## 1. Origins — Joseph Smith and the Founding Story

### The Official LDS Narrative

1. **First Vision (1820)**: 14-year-old Joseph Smith, confused about which
   church to join in Palmyra, New York, prayed in a grove and was visited by
   God the Father and Jesus Christ, who told him all existing churches were
   wrong.
2. **Angel Moroni (1823–1827)**: An angel named Moroni directed Smith to golden
   plates buried in a hill (Cumorah) near his home. Smith retrieved them in
   1827.
3. **Translation (1827–1829)**: Smith "translated" the plates by placing a
   "seer stone" in a hat and reading aloud while a scribe wrote. The
   resulting text: the **Book of Mormon**.
4. **Church Founded (1830)**: Smith organised the Church of Christ (later
   renamed) on 6 April 1830 in Fayette, New York.
5. **Westward Migration**: The church moved from New York → Ohio → Missouri →
   Illinois (Nauvoo). Smith was killed by a mob in Carthage, Illinois, on
   27 June 1844.
6. **Brigham Young and Utah**: Young led the majority of followers to the
   Salt Lake Valley in 1847, founding what became Utah.

### Historical Problems with the Founding Narrative

| Claim | Issue |
|---|---|
| **First Vision** | Smith told multiple, contradictory versions over the years. The 1832 account mentions only Jesus; the 1835 version mentions two "personages" plus angels; the canonical 1838 account adds God the Father. No contemporary record of the vision exists from 1820. (The LDS Church's own Gospel Topics Essays, published 2013–2014, acknowledge these discrepancies.) |
| **Golden plates** | No independent witness ever saw the plates uncovered. The "Three Witnesses" and "Eight Witnesses" reported in the Book of Mormon testified in spiritual/visionary terms. Martin Harris later said he saw them "with the eye of faith." |
| **Translation method** | Smith did not read the plates directly. Eyewitnesses describe him placing a "seer stone" (the same stone he previously used for treasure-hunting) in a hat and dictating — the plates were often not even in the room. The LDS Church acknowledged this in its 2013 essay "Book of Mormon Translation." |
| **Smith's background** | Before founding the church, Smith was a known "money digger" — people hired him to use his seer stones to locate buried treasure. In 1826, he was brought to trial in Bainbridge, New York, for this activity. Court records survive. |

## 2. The Book of Mormon — What Does the Evidence Say?

The Book of Mormon claims to be a record of Israelite peoples who migrated to
the Americas around 600 BCE and built major civilisations (Nephites and
Lamanites), culminating in Jesus visiting the Americas after his resurrection.

### Archaeological Evidence

**The verdict from mainstream archaeology and anthropology is clear: there is no
evidence supporting the Book of Mormon's historical claims.**

| Book of Mormon claims | Archaeological reality |
|---|---|
| Israelite peoples in the Americas (600 BCE–400 CE) | No evidence of Israelite-origin civilisations has ever been found in the Americas |
| **Horses** in pre-Columbian America | Horses went extinct in the Americas ~10,000 years ago and were reintroduced by the Spanish in the 1500s |
| **Steel swords** and metal weaponry | No pre-Columbian steel has been found; warfare technology was obsidian and stone |
| **Chariots** and wheeled vehicles | The wheel was not used for transportation in pre-Columbian Americas |
| **Wheat and barley** | These Old World crops were not present in the pre-Columbian Americas |
| **Elephants** (Ether 9:19) | Elephants (mammoths/mastodons) went extinct ~10,000 years ago |
| Cities like "Zarahemla" and "Bountiful" | No archaeological site in the Americas has been identified as a Book of Mormon location by mainstream archaeologists |
| A written language (reformed Egyptian) | No evidence of any Egyptian-derived writing system in the Americas |
| Millions of people in epic battles (e.g., the final battle at Cumorah with 230,000+ dead) | No battlefield, no mass graves, no weapons cache has been found at the Hill Cumorah in New York or anywhere else |

The **Smithsonian Institution** has issued a formal statement: "The Smithsonian
Institution has never used the Book of Mormon in any way as a scientific guide.
Smithsonian archaeologists see no direct connection between the archaeology of
the New World and the subject matter of the book."

The **National Geographic Society** has similarly stated it knows of no
archaeological evidence to support the Book of Mormon.

### DNA Evidence

In the early 2000s, DNA studies of Indigenous American populations dealt another
blow to Book of Mormon historicity:

- Genetic evidence overwhelmingly traces Indigenous American ancestry to
  **East Asia** (via the Bering land bridge, ~15,000+ years ago), **not** to
  the ancient Near East or Israel.
- Thomas Murphy (LDS anthropologist, excommunication proceedings dropped) and
  Simon Southerton (former LDS bishop, resigned) published analyses showing
  DNA evidence is incompatible with the Book of Mormon's claims.
- The LDS Church's 2014 Gospel Topics essay "Book of Mormon and DNA Studies"
  retreated from the traditional claim that Lehi's family were the *principal*
  ancestors of all Indigenous Americans, now suggesting they were a *small*
  group whose DNA could have been overwhelmed.

### 19th-Century Sources for the Book of Mormon

Scholars have identified plausible 19th-century sources for much of the Book of
Mormon's content:

- **View of the Hebrews** (1823) by Ethan Smith (no relation) — published
  7 years before the Book of Mormon, in the same region of Vermont. It
  proposed that Indigenous Americans descended from the lost tribes of Israel.
  B.H. Roberts, a General Authority of the LDS Church, studied this parallel
  extensively in the 1920s and concluded the similarities were deeply troubling
  (his manuscript, *Studies of the Book of Mormon*, was published posthumously
  in 1985).
- **The Late War Between the United States and Great Britain** (1816) — a
  schoolbook written in King James English with numerous narrative parallels
  to the Book of Mormon. Statistical analysis in 2013 found striking
  similarities in rare phrases.
- **King James Bible** — large sections of the Book of Mormon directly
  reproduce the KJV, including translation errors unique to the 1611 KJV that
  would not appear in a genuinely ancient text.
- **Contemporary 19th-century theology** — the Book of Mormon addresses debates
  active in 1820s New York (infant baptism, Trinitarianism vs. modalism, Free
  Masonry, anti-Catholicism) rather than concerns of ancient Mesoamerica.

## 3. The Book of Abraham — A Testable Case

This is perhaps the most damaging evidence against Joseph Smith's claims of
prophetic translation ability.

### The Story

In 1835, Smith purchased Egyptian mummies and papyri from a travelling
exhibitor. He declared one scroll to be written by Abraham's own hand and
"translated" it into the **Book of Abraham**, now LDS scripture (Pearl of Great
Price).

### What Happened Next

- The papyri were thought lost in the 1871 Chicago fire.
- In **1966**, fragments of the Joseph Smith Papyri were **rediscovered** in the
  Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York.
- Professional **Egyptologists** (both LDS and non-LDS) examined them and
  unanimously determined: the papyri are **common Egyptian funerary texts**
  (the "Book of Breathings," a late-period guide for the dead), dating to
  roughly 200 BCE–100 CE.
- The papyri have **nothing whatsoever to do with Abraham**. They are standard
  Egyptian religious documents.
- Smith's "Egyptian Alphabet and Grammar" (a notebook in which he attempted
  to match Egyptian characters to English meanings) is demonstrably wrong on
  virtually every character.
- The facsimiles Smith published (Facsimiles 1, 2, and 3) are standard
  Egyptian vignettes; his captions and explanations are incorrect according
  to every qualified Egyptologist.

### LDS Apologetic Response

The church's 2014 Gospel Topics essay on the Book of Abraham acknowledges
the papyri do not match Smith's translation and suggests Abraham might have
been a "catalyst" for revelation rather than a direct translation. This
effectively concedes the traditional claim.

### The Kinderhook Plates (1843)

A separate test: local men in Kinderhook, Illinois, fabricated brass plates
with nonsensical symbols and presented them to Smith. Smith reportedly began
"translating" them. The plates were **scientifically confirmed as 19th-century
forgeries** in 1980 (testing by the Chicago Historical Society). The LDS
Church's own *History of the Church* records Smith's engagement with the plates.

## 4. Other Historical Problems

### Polygamy

- Smith secretly practised polygamy from the early 1830s. He had between
  **30 and 40 wives**, including girls as young as 14 (Helen Mar Kimball) and
  women already married to living husbands ("polyandry").
- He publicly denied practising polygamy his entire life.
- The LDS Church's 2014 essay "Plural Marriage in Kirtland and Nauvoo"
  acknowledges these facts.
- Polygamy was publicly practised in Utah from 1852 until the 1890 Manifesto
  (banned under federal pressure and as a condition of Utah statehood).
- Some fundamentalist offshoot groups (FLDS) still practise polygamy today.

### Racism in Doctrine

- Until **1978**, Black men of African descent were denied the LDS priesthood
  and Black men and women were barred from temple ordinances.
- This was justified doctrinally: Brigham Young taught that Black skin was the
  "mark of Cain" and a curse. The Book of Mormon itself describes dark skin
  as a curse from God (2 Nephi 5:21).
- The 1978 "revelation" (Official Declaration 2) reversed the ban — but notably
  coincided with massive social pressure, potential loss of tax-exempt status
  (Bob Jones University precedent), and international expansion to Brazil.
- The church's 2013 essay "Race and the Priesthood" disavows past racial
  explanations but does **not** retract the Book of Mormon verses.

### The Book of Mormon's Internal Changes

The Book of Mormon has been revised **thousands of times** since its 1830 first
edition. Most are grammatical, but some are theologically significant:

- 1 Nephi 11:18 originally read: "the mother of **the Eternal Father**" →
  changed to "the mother of **the Son of** the Eternal Father" — shifting from
  modalism to a more Trinitarian view.
- Extensive grammar corrections suggest the text of an uneducated 19th-century
  man rather than a polished ancient record.

## 5. The LDS Church Today — Finances and Organisation

### Membership (Official, as of 31 Dec 2024)

- **Total membership**: 17,509,781 (reported at April 2025 General Conference)
- **Converts baptised in 2024**: 308,682
- **New children of record**: 91,617
- **Full-time missionaries**: 74,127
- **Total missionaries (incl. senior and service)**: 109,439
- **Congregations**: 31,676
- **Temples**: 16 dedicated in 2024; 8 under renovation

**Important caveat**: The LDS Church counts *anyone ever baptised* unless they
formally resign or are excommunicated. Activity rates are far lower. Researchers
estimate that **only 30–40% of members on the rolls are active** (attending
weekly). In many countries (especially Latin America and the Philippines), the
gap between claimed membership and active attendance is enormous.

### Finances

The LDS Church is one of the wealthiest religious organisations on Earth:

- **Ensign Peak Advisors**, the church's investment arm, manages an estimated
  **$100+ billion** in equities alone (some estimates put total church assets
  at $200–265 billion including real estate, farms, ranches, and businesses).
- In **2023**, the SEC charged Ensign Peak and the LDS Church for failing to
  file required disclosure forms (Form 13F) from 1997 to 2019, instead using
  **13 shell companies** to obscure the portfolio. Ensign Peak paid a
  **$4 million fine** and the church paid **$1 million**.
- The church requires **tithing** (10% of income) from members to be
  considered "worthy" for temple attendance. Tithing is a condition for
  marriage in the temple, which is required for the highest level of salvation
  ("exaltation") in LDS theology. This creates enormous financial pressure on
  members.
- There is **no public financial transparency**. The church does not publish
  audited financial statements.

### Retention and Youth Disaffiliation (2025 Data)

- **Pew Religious Landscape Study (2025)**: Latter-day Saints still have one of
  the highest church attendance rates and scripture-reading rates of any US
  religion.
- **However**, *Religion News Service* (Dec 2025) confirmed from nationally
  representative data (General Social Survey, 1973–2022; Pew RLS 2023–24)
  that **US Gen Z and Millennial Latter-day Saints are leaving at
  significantly higher rates** than older generations.
- The LDS Church was once "legendary for its impressive retention rates among
  young people" (sociologist Christian Smith). That era appears to be ending.
- Those who stay are **far more devout** than average Americans their age — the
  middle is hollowing out.

## 6. Why Do People Believe? — A Sociological and Psychological Analysis

Your question — how is it even *possible* that so many people believe this? —
is one of the most important questions to ask. The answer has nothing to do with
evidence and everything to do with how human belief works.

### 1. Born Into It (Most Members)

The majority of LDS members were **raised in the faith**. They were taught the
church was true before they could critically evaluate it. Children are baptised
at 8 — too young for informed consent. The church actively discourages members
from reading "anti-Mormon" material.

### 2. Community and Identity

The LDS Church provides **an extraordinarily tight-knit community**: mutual aid,
social events, youth programmes, family history resources, and a defined life
path (mission → temple marriage → children → church service). Leaving means
losing your social world, often including family relationships.

### 3. Emotional Epistemology

Mormonism teaches a specific method for determining truth: **pray about it, and
if you feel a "burning in the bosom" (a warm feeling), it's true** (Moroni
10:4–5). This creates a closed epistemological loop:

- Feel good when praying about the Book of Mormon → it's true.
- Feel doubt or encounter contrary evidence → that's Satan testing you.
- This emotional method is indistinguishable from the confirmation bias every
  human brain is subject to. Hindus, Muslims, and Evangelicals all report
  identical spiritual confirmations of *their* beliefs.

### 4. Sunk Cost and Social Pressure

Members invest enormous amounts: **10% of lifetime income** (tithing),
**2 years** of youth (mission), time in callings, temple ceremonies, and
emotional energy. Walking away means acknowledging that investment was based
on false premises — psychologically devastating.

### 5. Information Control

Until the internet era, the church successfully controlled the narrative. Most
members had no idea about:
- Multiple First Vision accounts
- Smith's treasure-digging background
- The Book of Abraham papyri problem
- Smith's true number of wives (including teenagers and other men's wives)
- The full history of the priesthood ban

The internet has shattered this control. The **CES Letter** (2013), a document
compiling historical problems written by Jeremy Runnels, has been credited with
triggering tens of thousands of faith crises. The church's own Gospel Topics
Essays (2013–2014) were a direct response to the information crisis.

### 6. It's Not Unique — All Religions Work This Way

The mechanisms that make Mormonism believable are the same ones that sustain
*every* religion:

- Childhood indoctrination
- Community belonging
- Emotional confirmation mistaken for divine witness
- Social cost of leaving
- Selective exposure to information

Mormonism is simply **more visible** because it's young enough (< 200 years)
that we have comprehensive historical records to evaluate its claims in real
time — unlike, say, claims made 2,000+ years ago about Christianity, where
evidence is scarcer and harder to assess.

## 7. Fact vs. Fiction Scorecard — Mormonism

### ESTABLISHED FACTS (scholarly/scientific consensus)

1. Joseph Smith existed and founded the LDS Church in 1830.
2. He had a documented career as a "money digger" and treasure seeker before
   founding the church.
3. The Book of Mormon was dictated (not read from plates) using a seer stone
   in a hat.
4. Multiple contradictory accounts of the First Vision exist.
5. The Book of Abraham papyri are standard Egyptian funerary texts; Smith's
   "translation" is completely wrong.
6. The Kinderhook Plates were a hoax that Smith engaged with.
7. DNA evidence overwhelmingly traces Indigenous Americans to East Asia, not
   Israel.
8. No archaeological evidence supports any Book of Mormon civilisation, city,
   person, or event.
9. The Smithsonian Institution and National Geographic Society have formally
   denied any connection to the Book of Mormon.
10. Smith practised polygamy with 30–40 wives, including teenagers and married
    women, while publicly denying it.
11. The church banned Black people from the priesthood and temple until 1978.
12. The LDS Church manages $100+ billion through Ensign Peak and was fined by
    the SEC in 2023 for concealing its portfolio.

### WHAT THE LDS CHURCH ITSELF NOW ACKNOWLEDGES (via Gospel Topics Essays)

- Multiple First Vision accounts with differences
- Seer stone used in "translation"
- Book of Abraham papyri don't match the "translation"
- DNA doesn't support traditional claims about Indigenous American ancestry
- Joseph Smith's polygamy, including marriages to teenagers
- Historical racial doctrines were wrong

### WHAT REMAINS UNSUPPORTED

| Claim | Status |
|---|---|
| Golden plates existed as a physical ancient record | No independent evidence; described only in visionary terms by witnesses |
| The Book of Mormon is an ancient historical record | Zero archaeological, genetic, or linguistic support |
| Joseph Smith was a prophet who received revelation from God | An unfalsifiable faith claim |
| The LDS Church is the "only true church" on Earth | A theological claim, not a historical one |
| Temple ordinances (baptism for the dead, etc.) have spiritual efficacy | Untestable |

## 8. Comparison: Mormonism vs. Mainstream Christianity

| Dimension | Mainstream Christianity | Mormonism (LDS) |
|---|---|---|
| **Founding figure historicity** | Jesus almost certainly existed (broad scholarly consensus) | Joseph Smith certainly existed (documented) |
| **Core text** | Gospels written 35–70 years after Jesus, anonymous, in a different language | Book of Mormon dictated by one person in 1829, in English, with identifiable 19th-century sources |
| **Archaeological support** | Numerous: Dead Sea Scrolls, Pilate Stone, Bethsaida, Pool of Siloam, etc. | None. Zero sites, artefacts, or writing systems confirmed |
| **Testable claims** | Few directly testable supernatural claims; historical context confirmed | Multiple directly testable claims — all have **failed testing** (Book of Abraham, DNA, archaeology, Kinderhook Plates) |
| **Time buffer** | 2,000 years of distance make evaluation harder | <200 years — full documentary record available |
| **Scholarly consensus** | Jesus existed; Christianity emerged from real historical events (however interpreted) | Book of Mormon has no historical basis (mainstream archaeology, genetics, Egyptology) |

This comparison is important: **Christianity benefits from antiquity**. The
further back in time a religion originates, the harder it is to disprove (or
prove) its claims. Mormonism is uniquely vulnerable because its foundational
claims are modern, specific, and **testable** — and they fail those tests.

---

## 9. Bottom Line

Mormonism is a remarkable sociological phenomenon. In less than 200 years, a
religion founded by a single individual with a documented history of folk magic
and treasure-digging has grown to 17.5 million claimed members, amassed over
$100 billion in investments, built 300+ temples worldwide, and produced US
presidential candidates.

**How?** Not because the evidence supports it — it doesn't. But because
religions don't primarily spread through evidence. They spread through:

- Family and community
- Emotional experiences mistaken for divine confirmation
- Social structures that reward loyalty and punish doubt
- Enormous institutional investment (missionaries, temples, media)
- The basic human need for meaning, belonging, and certainty

This is not unique to Mormonism. It's how religion works. Mormonism is just
easier to study because the historical record is so complete.
