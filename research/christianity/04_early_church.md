# The Early Church — First Three Centuries

## From Jewish Sect to World Religion

The transformation of a small Jewish messianic movement into a Gentile-majority,
empire-wide religion is one of the most consequential developments in Western
history — and one of the best documented for the ancient world.

---

## 1. The Jerusalem Church (c. 30–70 CE)

### What We Know (from Paul and Acts)

- Leadership: **James (brother of Jesus)**, Peter, and John — the "pillars"
  (Gal 2:9).
- The earliest followers were **observant Jews** who continued attending the
  Temple and keeping Torah.
- They believed Jesus was the Messiah who would return imminently.
- Communal living and sharing of possessions (Acts 2:44–45 — idealized but
  probably reflects some communal practice).
- **The Jerusalem Council** (c. 49 CE; Acts 15, Gal 2) — pivotal meeting
  deciding that Gentile converts did **not** need to be circumcised or fully
  observe Torah.

### The Paul–James Tension

Paul and James represent two poles of early Christianity:
- **Paul**: Gentiles are justified by faith in Christ, not by Torah observance.
- **James**: faith without works is dead (James 2:14–26); continued Torah
  observance.

This is not a minor disagreement — it reflects a fundamental debate about
the identity of the movement.

### Fall of Jerusalem (70 CE)

The Roman destruction of the Temple effectively ended the Jerusalem church's
centrality. Christianity's centre of gravity shifted to Gentile cities
(Antioch, Ephesus, Rome, Alexandria).

## 2. Spread and Growth (c. 50–300 CE)

### Factors Behind Christianity's Growth

Sociologist Rodney Stark (*The Rise of Christianity*, 1996) and historian
Keith Hopkins estimate Christianity grew from ~1,000 adherents in 40 CE to
~6 million by 300 CE (~40% growth rate per decade). Contributing factors:

1. **Social networks** — spread along trade routes and kinship/patron networks.
2. **Urban appeal** — Christianity offered community, mutual aid, and dignity
   to urban poor, women, and slaves.
3. **Plague response** — Christians cared for the sick during epidemics
   (Antonine Plague 165 CE, Plague of Cyprian 250 CE), earning respect and
   converts.
4. **Inclusive monotheism** — unlike Judaism, no circumcision barrier for males;
   unlike paganism, offered a single coherent theology and afterlife hope.
5. **Martyrdom** — public executions sometimes backfired, generating sympathy
   ("the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church" — Tertullian).

### Persecutions — Fact vs. Exaggeration

- Nero (64 CE) — targeted Christians in Rome after the Great Fire. Tacitus
  confirms this (*Annals* 15.44).
- Sporadic local persecutions through the 2nd–3rd centuries (Pliny's letter to
  Trajan, c. 112 CE).
- Empire-wide systematic persecution was **rare** before Decius (250 CE) and
  Diocletian (303–311 CE).
- **The popular image of 300 years of constant persecution is exaggerated.**
  Candida Moss (*The Myth of Persecution*, 2013) documents how later
  hagiography inflated and invented martyr stories.

## 3. Theological Diversity

Early Christianity was **far more diverse** than later orthodoxy would
suggest. Major groups:

| Group | Key beliefs | Period |
|---|---|---|
| **Jewish Christians** (Ebionites, Nazarenes) | Jesus was Messiah but not divine; Torah still binding | 1st–4th c. |
| **Pauline Christians** | Faith in Christ supersedes Torah; high Christology developing | 1st c. onward |
| **Gnostics** (Valentinians, Sethians, Marcionites) | Material world is evil; secret knowledge (*gnosis*) saves; the God of the OT is a lesser or evil deity | 2nd–4th c. |
| **Docetists** | Jesus only *appeared* to have a physical body | 2nd c. onward |
| **Montanists** | New prophecy; ecstatic worship; imminent end-times | 2nd–3rd c. |
| **Proto-orthodox** | Would eventually become "mainstream" Catholicism/Orthodoxy | 2nd c. onward |

> **Fact**: There was no single, unified "Christianity" in the first two
> centuries. What became "orthodoxy" won out through a combination of
> institutional power, theological argument, and political circumstance — not
> through an unbroken chain from Jesus.

## 4. Development of Church Structure

- **1st century**: charismatic, decentralised; apostles, prophets, teachers.
- **Early 2nd century**: three-fold ministry emerging — bishop (*episkopos*),
  presbyter/elder (*presbyteros*), deacon (*diakonos*). See Ignatius of Antioch
  (c. 110 CE).
- **3rd century**: bishop of Rome claimed increasing authority; other major sees
  (Alexandria, Antioch, Constantinople) also wielded power.
- The *monarchical bishop* model eventually prevailed, setting the stage for
  the papal system.

## 5. Key Primary Sources

- **Paul's Letters** (c. 50–60 CE) — earliest Christian documents.
- **Didache** (c. 50–120 CE) — early church order.
- **1 Clement** (c. 96 CE) — Roman church to Corinth.
- **Ignatius of Antioch** (c. 110 CE) — episcopal authority, anti-docetism.
- **Justin Martyr** (c. 150 CE) — Christian apologist; describes worship
  practices.
- **Irenaeus of Lyon** (*Against Heresies*, c. 180 CE) — combats Gnosticism;
  establishes four-Gospel canon.
- **Tertullian** (c. 200 CE) — Latin theology; coined "Trinity" in Latin
  (*trinitas*).
- **Origen** (c. 185–254 CE) — allegorical interpretation; towering
  intellectual.
- **Eusebius of Caesarea** (*Church History*, c. 313 CE) — first church
  historian; invaluable but biased toward the winners.

## 6. Recent Archaeological Evidence for Early Christianity's Spread (2024–2025)

- **Silver Amulet, Frankfurt (c. 230–260 CE)** — Found in late 2024, this is
  the **oldest evidence of Christianity north of the Alps**. Inscribed: "Holy,
  holy, holy! In the name of Jesus Christ, Son of God!" Shows Christianity
  had reached the Germanic frontier within 200 years of Jesus' death.
  (Source: Archaeological Museum Frankfurt, 2024)

- **Plaster Cross at Sir Bani Yas Island, Abu Dhabi (2025)** — A 1,400-year-old
  plaster cross was discovered near a seventh/eighth-century monastic complex,
  confirming the spread of Christianity in the Persian Gulf region **prior to
  the advent of Islam**. (Source: Dept. of Culture and Tourism, Abu Dhabi, 2025;
  *National Geographic*, Dec 2025)

- **Mosaic at Olympos, Turkey (2025)** — At the ancient city of Olympos,
  archaeologists uncovered a fifth-century church entrance mosaic declaring:
  "Only those on the right path may enter here." (Source: *Türkiye Today*, 2025)

- **African-Style Figurines in Negev Christian Graves (2025)** — Ebony
  figurines in a Negev Desert Christian burial (4th–7th c. CE) suggest
  connections to Ethiopia, illustrating African–Levantine Christian networks.
  (Source: *Atiqot*, Israel Antiquities Authority, 2025)

- **Megiddo Mosaic Church Floor in the US (2025)** — The earliest known
  Christian church floor (Megiddo prison mosaic, 3rd c. CE), discovered in
  2005, was displayed at the Bible Museum in 2025 after being removed from its
  site near a Roman legion camp. The mosaic, which references "the God Jesus
  Christ," is among the oldest material evidence of Christian worship.
  (Source: *Christianity Today*, Jan 2025)

- **Roman Roads Map (Itiner-e, 2025)** — An international team mapped 14,769
  Roman road segments (185,896 miles), offering unprecedented clarity on the
  infrastructure that enabled apostolic missions like Paul's journeys.
  (Source: *Nature Scientific Data*, 2025)
