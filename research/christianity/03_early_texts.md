# Early Christian Texts — The New Testament and Beyond

## Overview

The New Testament contains 27 books written roughly between 50 and 120 CE. None
were written by Jesus himself. Understanding *who* wrote them, *when*, and *why*
is essential for evaluating their historical reliability.

---

## 1. Paul's Letters (c. 50–60 CE) — The Earliest Christian Documents

Paul of Tarsus wrote letters to early Christian communities **before** any
Gospel was composed. Seven letters are virtually unanimously accepted as
authentic:

| Letter | Approx. date | Key content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 Thessalonians | c. 50 CE | Earliest surviving Christian document; imminent return of Jesus |
| Galatians | c. 50–55 | Justification by faith; Paul met James and Peter |
| 1 Corinthians | c. 53–55 | Resurrection creed (15:3–7); church problems |
| 2 Corinthians | c. 55–56 | Paul's apostolic defence |
| Romans | c. 57 | Paul's theological masterpiece |
| Philippians | c. 55–62 | Christ hymn (2:6–11); possibly pre-Pauline |
| Philemon | c. 55–62 | Personal letter about a slave/freedman |

**Disputed / Deutero-Pauline** (likely written by followers in Paul's name):
Ephesians, Colossians, 2 Thessalonians.

**Almost certainly pseudonymous** (forged in Paul's name):
1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus (the "Pastoral Epistles") — vocabulary, theology,
and church structure point to the early second century.

> **Fact**: Pseudonymous writing (writing under a famous person's name) was
> common in antiquity and not always considered dishonest. However, it means
> these letters do not represent Paul's own views.

### What Paul Tells Us About Jesus

Paul says surprisingly little about Jesus' earthly life:
- Jesus was "born of a woman, born under the law" (Gal 4:4)
- He had a brother named James (Gal 1:19)
- He instituted a meal ritual (1 Cor 11:23–26)
- He was crucified (1 Cor 2:2)
- He appeared to Peter, the Twelve, 500+ people, James, then Paul (1 Cor 15:5–8)

Paul **never** mentions: virgin birth, Bethlehem, miracles, parables, Pontius
Pilate by name (in his undisputed letters), or an empty tomb.

## 2. The Gospels

### The Synoptic Problem

Matthew, Mark, and Luke share extensive material. The dominant scholarly solution:

- **Mark** was written first (c. 65–75 CE).
- Matthew and Luke independently used Mark **plus** a lost sayings source called
  **Q** (from German *Quelle*, "source").
- Matthew and Luke each added their own unique material (M and L sources).

### Gospel Dating and Authorship

| Gospel | Approx. date | Traditional author | Scholarly consensus on authorship |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mark** | c. 65–75 CE | John Mark | Anonymous; tradition links to Peter's interpreter |
| **Matthew** | c. 80–90 CE | Matthew the tax collector | Anonymous; used Mark (unlikely if author was an eyewitness) |
| **Luke** | c. 80–90 CE | Luke the physician | Anonymous; shares theology with Acts |
| **John** | c. 90–100 CE | John son of Zebedee | Anonymous; "beloved disciple" tradition; heavily theological |

> **Fact**: All four Gospels are formally **anonymous**. The names "according to
> Matthew" etc. were added later (attested from the mid-second century). This is
> the consensus of mainstream scholarship, not a fringe position.

### Genre

The Gospels are ancient Greco-Roman *bioi* (biographical literature) — not
modern biographies or newspaper reports. They combine historical memory,
theological interpretation, and literary artistry.

## 3. Other New Testament Books

- **Acts of the Apostles** — by the same author as Luke; history of the early
  church (with significant theological shaping).
- **Hebrews** — anonymous homily; author unknown (not Paul).
- **James, 1–2 Peter, 1–3 John, Jude** — called "Catholic/General Epistles";
  authorship debated for most.
- **Revelation (Apocalypse)** — by "John" (likely not the same John as the
  Gospel); apocalyptic literature written c. 90–96 CE.

---

## 4. Non-Canonical Early Christian Texts

Important texts not included in the New Testament:

- **Didache** (c. 50–120 CE) — early church manual.
- **Gospel of Thomas** (c. 60–140 CE, debated) — sayings gospel; no narrative;
  some sayings may be independent of the canonical Gospels.
- **1 Clement** (c. 96 CE) — letter from the Roman church to Corinth.
- **Letters of Ignatius** (c. 110 CE) — early bishop; shows developing church
  hierarchy.
- **Gospel of Peter** — fragmentary; docetic tendencies.
- **Infancy Gospel of Thomas** — miracle stories of the child Jesus (clearly
  legendary).
- **Nag Hammadi Library** (discovered 1945) — Gnostic texts shedding light on
  Christian diversity in the 2nd–4th centuries.

---

## 5. How the Canon Was Formed

- There was **no official New Testament canon for the first 300+ years**.
- Early Christians used different collections of texts depending on region.
- Marcion (c. 140 CE) proposed a reduced canon (edited Luke + 10 Pauline
  letters), prompting the broader church to define its own.
- Athanasius' Easter Letter of **367 CE** is the first known list matching the
  modern 27-book canon.
- Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) ratified the list.

**Criteria the early church used** (not formal, but reconstructed):
1. Apostolic origin (or connection to an apostle)
2. Widespread use in churches
3. Theological consistency ("rule of faith")

> **Fact**: The canon was a gradual, human, historically conditioned process —
> not a single divine decree.

## 6. Recent Developments in Manuscript Studies (2024–2025)

- **Magdalen Papyrus (P64) Reassessment** — Oxford's Magdalen College holds
  fragments of Matthew's Gospel (P64) once controversially dated to the first
  century by Carsten Thiede (1995). The current scholarly consensus, confirmed
  by ongoing palaeographic work, places them in the **late second century**
  (c. 200 CE). They remain among the dozen earliest surviving New Testament
  fragments and the oldest known codex (book-form) fragments in Oxford's
  collections. (Source: Magdalen College, Oxford, updated 2025)

- **AI-Assisted Dead Sea Scroll Dating** — The "Enoch" AI model (University of
  Groningen, 2025) re-dated multiple Dead Sea Scroll manuscripts. Most matched
  earlier palaeographic estimates, but fragment 4Q114 (Daniel) was dated to
  230–160 BCE — potentially older than previously thought. While this does not
  settle the authorship or prophecy debate, it opens new avenues for
  computational palaeography. (Source: *PLOS ONE*, 2025)

- **AI Unlocks Cairo Geniza Texts (2025)** — Hundreds of thousands of pieces
  of medieval Jewish texts from the Cairo synagogue *geniza* have been
  digitised, and AI is now accelerating their translation. Most have never
  been read or studied. (Source: *Reuters*, Nov 2025)

- **St. Catherine's Monastery Manuscripts at Risk (2025)** — Egypt nationalised
  St. Catherine's Monastery (Sinai), the oldest continuously operating
  monastery and home to one of the world's most important collections of early
  Christian manuscripts. After international pressure, Egypt agreed to
  "guarantee the character of the monastery in perpetuity."
  (Source: *Reuters*, Oct 2025; *Christianity Today*, 2025)

- **Newly Analysed NT Fragments Stir Debate** — A set of newly analysed New
  Testament fragments (reported on social media and academic channels in 2025)
  is sparking fresh discussion about how the earliest Jesus sayings were
  recorded, copied, and transmitted. Full publication is awaited.
