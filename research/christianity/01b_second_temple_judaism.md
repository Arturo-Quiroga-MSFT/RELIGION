# Second Temple Judaism — The World That Produced Christianity

## Why This Matters

Christianity did not emerge in a vacuum. It was one of many movements within
the extraordinarily diverse world of Second Temple Judaism (c. 516 BCE–70 CE).
Understanding these groups in depth is essential for two reasons:

1. **Jesus' teachings are unintelligible without this context.** His debates
   about the Torah, his resurrection theology, his apocalyptic language — all
   of it was already being argued about by Jewish groups before he was born.
2. **Christianity's claim to uniqueness depends on how different it actually
   was.** Spoiler: less different than most Christians realize.

This file covers the six major groups/currents in first-century Judaism:
Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots/Sicarii, Samaritans, and the
Apocalypticist tradition — plus the messianic claimants who preceded, followed,
and surrounded Jesus.

---

## 1. The Pharisees

### What We Know

The Pharisees are the most important group for understanding both Jesus and the
survival of Judaism after 70 CE. Our sources are:

- **Josephus** (*Antiquities* 13, 17, 18; *Jewish War* 2): Josephus calls them
  one of three "philosophies" of the Jews (alongside Sadducees and Essenes). He
  reports approximately **6,000 Pharisees** during the reign of Herod the
  Great — a small number, but disproportionately influential.
- **The New Testament** (especially Matthew 23, Mark 7, Acts 23): Hostile
  portrait — "hypocrites," "blind guides." This reflects inter-Jewish polemic,
  not objective reporting. *By the time the Gospels were written (70–100 CE),
  the Pharisees were the dominant surviving Jewish group, and the Jesus movement
  was competing with them for the same audience.*
- **Rabbinic literature** (Mishnah, Talmud): The rabbis traced their lineage
  back to the Pharisees, but rabbinic texts were compiled centuries later
  (Mishnah c. 200 CE, Talmud c. 400–600 CE). They are retrospective and
  idealized.

### Core Beliefs and Practices

| Belief/Practice | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Oral Torah** | The Pharisees held that Moses received not only the written Torah (Pentateuch) but also an **oral Torah** — interpretive traditions passed down through generations. This became the foundation of later Rabbinic Judaism. |
| **Resurrection of the dead** | Believed in bodily resurrection at the end of the age — a belief shared by Jesus and Paul, and explicitly rejected by the Sadducees (Acts 23:6–8). |
| **Angels and spirits** | Accepted an elaborate angelology and demonology, consistent with apocalyptic literature. Sadducees rejected this. |
| **Providence and free will** | Josephus says the Pharisees believed in a combination of divine providence and human free will — "fate cooperates in every action" (*Ant.* 18.13). |
| **Synagogue-based** | While they respected the Temple, the Pharisees emphasized study, prayer, and communal observance in synagogues — which made them the only group that could **survive the Temple's destruction** in 70 CE. |
| **Purity beyond the Temple** | Extended Temple purity rules to everyday life (hand-washing, tithing, food laws). This is exactly what Jesus debates with them in Mark 7. |

### The Pharisees and Jesus — What Scholars Now Think

The Gospel portrait of the Pharisees as Jesus' primary enemies is **heavily
influenced by later Christian-Jewish conflict** (especially post-70 CE). Modern
scholarship emphasizes:

- Jesus was **closer to the Pharisees** than to any other group. He shared
  their belief in resurrection, their synagogue-based teaching, their
  apocalyptic expectations, and their emphasis on ethical Torah interpretation.
- The debates in the Gospels (Sabbath, purity, divorce) are **intra-Pharisaic**
  in character — they resemble the later debates between the schools of Hillel
  and Shammai recorded in the Mishnah.
- Paul identifies himself as a Pharisee: "a Pharisee, son of Pharisees" (Acts
  23:6; Philippians 3:5).
- The hostility in Matthew 23 ("Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees,
  hypocrites!") reflects the situation c. 80–90 CE, when Matthew's community
  was being expelled from synagogues, not the situation during Jesus' lifetime.

**Key scholars**: E.P. Sanders (*Judaism: Practice and Belief*), Jacob
Neusner (*The Rabbinic Traditions About the Pharisees Before 70*), Anthony
Saldarini (*Pharisees, Scribes and Sadducees in Palestinian Society*).

### What Happened to Them

After the Temple's destruction in 70 CE, the Pharisees — under the leadership
of **Rabban Yohanan ben Zakkai** — reorganized Judaism around Torah study,
prayer, and synagogue life at **Yavneh** (Jamnia). This became **Rabbinic
Judaism**, the ancestor of all modern Jewish denominations. Every other
first-century Jewish group either vanished (Sadducees, Zealots) or became
marginal (Samaritans).

## 2. The Sadducees

### What We Know

The Sadducees are paradoxically one of the most powerful and least understood
groups: we have **no surviving texts written by Sadducees**. Everything we know
comes from their opponents (Josephus, the New Testament, rabbinic literature).

- The name probably derives from **Zadok** (the high priest under David and
  Solomon, 2 Samuel 8:17). They claimed descent from the legitimate high-
  priestly line.
- Josephus describes them as a "philosophy" attractive to the wealthy and
  powerful: "the Sadducees are able to persuade none but the rich" (*Ant.*
  13.298).

### Core Beliefs

| Belief | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Written Torah only** | Rejected the Pharisees' oral Torah. Only the five books of Moses (Pentateuch) had divine authority. |
| **No resurrection** | Denied bodily resurrection — a position explicitly noted in Mark 12:18, Acts 23:8, and Josephus. This is the key theological divide with the Pharisees. |
| **No angels or spirits** | Acts 23:8 states they denied angels and spirits. This is debated (the Pentateuch clearly mentions angels), but they likely rejected the elaborate angelology of Second Temple apocalyptic texts. |
| **No afterlife** | The soul perishes with the body (Josephus, *Jewish War* 2.165). Reward and punishment exist only in this life. |
| **Temple-centred** | Their identity, authority, and economic power were entirely tied to the Temple cult. The high priest was typically Sadducean. |
| **Political pragmatism** | Accommodated Roman rule to preserve their position. Collaborated with Rome to maintain order — and their own privilege. |

### The Sadducees and Jesus

- The Sadducees test Jesus with the resurrection riddle (Mark 12:18–27) — a
  married woman with seven dead husbands, whose wife would she be at
  the resurrection? Jesus' answer aligns with Pharisaic belief.
- The high priest **Caiaphas** (who presided over Jesus' trial) was almost
  certainly Sadducean. His ossuary was discovered in Jerusalem in 1990.
- The conflict between Jesus and the Temple authorities (the "cleansing" of the
  Temple, Mark 11) was a conflict with the Sadducean power structure, not with
  the Pharisees.

### What Happened to Them

**They vanished after 70 CE.** With no Temple, they had no sacrifice, no
priesthood, no power base, and no theological apparatus for survival. Their
disappearance is one of the clearest examples of a religious group that failed
to adapt to catastrophic change.

## 3. The Essenes and the Dead Sea Scrolls

### What We Know

The Essenes are described by three ancient writers:

- **Josephus** (*Jewish War* 2.119–161): Gives the most detailed account — a
  communal, celibate (mostly), ascetic group practising ritual immersion,
  communal meals, and strict purity. About **4,000 members** across Judea.
- **Philo of Alexandria** (*Every Good Man Is Free* 75–91): Emphasizes their
  communal property and rejection of slavery.
- **Pliny the Elder** (*Natural History* 5.73): Describes a group living
  near the western shore of the Dead Sea, "above Ein Gedi" — a geographic
  marker that fits Qumran.

### The Dead Sea Scrolls and the Qumran Debate

**The Dead Sea Scrolls** (~900 manuscripts, discovered 1947–1956 in caves near
Khirbet Qumran) are the most important manuscript discovery of the 20th
century. They include:

- **Biblical manuscripts**: Every book of the Hebrew Bible except Esther.
  Predate the oldest previously known Hebrew manuscripts by ~1,000 years.
- **Sectarian texts**: The *Rule of the Community* (1QS), the *War Scroll*
  (1QM), the *Temple Scroll* (11QT), the *Damascus Document* (CD), and the
  *Pesharim* (commentaries applying scripture to the sect's own history).
- **Apocalyptic texts**: 1 Enoch (Aramaic fragments), Jubilees, the *Songs of
  the Sabbath Sacrifice*.

**The standard hypothesis** (de Vaux, Cross, Vermes) is that the scrolls were
produced by an Essene community living at Qumran. The match is strong:

- Communal life, ritual bathing, strict purity, celibacy (or restricted
  marriage), communal property — all described in both Josephus and the
  *Rule of the Community*.
- Geographic fit with Pliny's description.
- The site has ritual baths (*miqva'ot*), a communal dining hall, and a
  scriptorium (a writing room with inkwells and plastered writing tables).

**Alternative hypotheses** (debate is ongoing):

| Theory | Proponents | Argument |
|---|---|---|
| **Essene hypothesis** (majority view) | de Vaux, Cross, Vermes, VanderKam | The scrolls belong to the Essene community at Qumran |
| **Roman manor house / villa** | Hirschfeld, Magen & Peleg | Qumran was a secular site (pottery factory, villa); scrolls were brought from Jerusalem |
| **Sadducean origin** | Schiffman | Some legal positions in the scrolls (especially 4QMMT) align with Sadducean halakha, not Essene |
| **Jerusalem library** | Golb | The scrolls represent diverse Jewish libraries hidden during the war of 66–73 CE |

**Current state of debate**: The Essene hypothesis remains the majority view
but is no longer unanimous. The key problem: **no scroll or fragment directly
names the Essenes**, and no archaeological artifact at Qumran directly links
the site to the sect. The connection is inferential — strong, but indirect.

### The Teacher of Righteousness

The sectarian scrolls refer repeatedly to a figure called the **Teacher of
Righteousness** (*moreh ha-tsedeq*) — a priestly leader who founded or
reorganized the community after a conflict with the **Wicked Priest** (probably
a Hasmonean high priest, most likely Jonathan or Simon Maccabee, mid-2nd
century BCE).

- The Teacher is **never named** in the scrolls.
- He is presented as a divinely inspired interpreter of scripture, not a
  messiah.
- The community believed it was living in the "last days" and awaited **two
  messiahs**: a priestly messiah (of Aaron) and a royal messiah (of Israel/
  David). This is unique — most Jewish groups expected only one.

### The Essenes and Christianity — Connections and Limits

Parallels between the Essenes/Qumran community and early Christianity are
striking but must be handled carefully:

| Parallel | Substance |
|---|---|
| Communal property | Acts 2:44–45 describes early Christians sharing possessions — similar to Qumran's communal ownership |
| Ritual immersion | John the Baptist and early Christian baptism resemble (but are not identical to) Qumran's daily ablutions |
| Apocalyptic expectation | Both groups believed they were living in the last days before God's intervention |
| Messianic hope | Both awaited a messiah — but Qumran expected two (priestly + royal), Christians claimed one who fulfilled both roles |
| "New covenant" language | Both use the phrase "new covenant" (Jeremiah 31:31) — Qumran in the Damascus Document, Paul in 1 Corinthians 11:25 |
| Teacher figure | Both centred on a founding teacher, but the Teacher of Righteousness was not a messiah and there is no resurrection claim associated with him |

**What scholars conclude**: The parallels reflect a **shared Jewish
apocalyptic milieu**, not direct borrowing or institutional connection.
John the Baptist may have had some contact with Essene circles (he operated in
the Jordan wilderness, near Qumran), but this is speculation. There is no
evidence that Jesus was an Essene or that Christianity derived from the Qumran
community.

## 4. The Zealots and the Sicarii

### Terminology Problem

Modern usage lumps "Zealots" into a single anti-Roman resistance movement, but
the sources distinguish several groups:

| Group | Description |
|---|---|
| **"Fourth Philosophy"** | Josephus' term for the ideology started by **Judas of Galilee** (6 CE) who led a tax revolt, arguing that paying tribute to Rome was treason against God. Josephus blames this ideology for eventually causing the war. |
| **Zealots** (zēlōtai) | A specific faction that emerged **during** the revolt of 66–73 CE. Led by figures like Eleazar ben Simon. Seized control of the Temple. Not identical to the Fourth Philosophy. |
| **Sicarii** (Latin: "dagger-men") | Assassins who used concealed daggers (*sicae*) to kill Roman collaborators in crowds. Led by **Menahem ben Judah** (grandson of Judas of Galilee) and later **Eleazar ben Ya'ir** (Masada). |

**Key point**: These were **not** a single organized movement but overlapping
factions with different leaders, strategies, and theological commitments. They
spent much of the revolt **fighting each other** as well as the Romans.

### The First Jewish–Roman War (66–73 CE)

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **66 CE** | Revolt begins: Jewish militants defeat the Syrian legion at Beth Horon. Cessation of sacrifice for the Emperor in the Temple (an act of political rebellion). |
| **67–68 CE** | Vespasian's campaign in Galilee. Josephus captured at Jotapata and defects to Rome. |
| **68–69 CE** | Roman civil war ("Year of the Four Emperors") pauses the campaign. |
| **69 CE** | Jewish factions fight a brutal civil war inside Jerusalem. Zealots, Sicarii, and Idumaeans destroy each other's food supplies. |
| **70 CE** | **Titus besieges and destroys Jerusalem and the Second Temple** (August/September). Josephus' account (*Jewish War* 5–6) describes famine, cannibalism, mass crucifixion, and the razing of the city. |
| **73/74 CE** | Fall of **Masada** — last Sicarii stronghold. According to Josephus, 960 defenders chose mass suicide rather than Roman capture. (See note below.) |

### Masada — Fact and Myth

Josephus (*Jewish War* 7.389–401) claims 960 people committed mass suicide.
Modern scholars are divided:

- **Archaeological evidence** (Yigael Yadin's 1963–1965 excavation): Found the
  Roman siege ramp, camp outlines, storehouses, synagogue — but **only 28
  skeletal remains** in the entire site. Not 960.
- **Josephus was not present** and attributed his account to two surviving
  women. His speeches are literary compositions in the ancient historiographic
  tradition — not transcripts.
- **Some historians** (Shaye Cohen, Kenneth Atkinson) argue the mass suicide
  narrative may be Josephus' literary creation, drawing on Greco-Roman models
  of "noble death." Others accept it as substantially historical.

Masada became a powerful national symbol in modern Israel ("Masada shall not
fall again!"), but the scholarly picture is more complicated than the myth.

### The Bar Kokhba Revolt (132–136 CE) — The Final Act

Two generations later, another revolt erupted:

- **Simon bar Kokhba** (bar Koseva) led a massive revolt against Emperor
  Hadrian, triggered by Hadrian's ban on circumcision and his plan to build
  a pagan temple (Jupiter Capitolinus) on the Temple Mount.
- **Rabbi Akiva** — the most prominent scholar of the era — declared bar Kokhba
  the **Messiah**. This is the single most authoritative messianic endorsement
  in Jewish history outside of Christianity.
- Bar Kokhba initially **succeeded**: he ruled an independent state for roughly
  three years, minted coins, and administered territory. Letters in his own
  hand were discovered in the Cave of Letters (Nahal Hever, 1960–1961).
- The Romans sent **12 legions** (an extraordinary commitment) under Julius
  Severus. The revolt ended with the fall of **Betar** (135 CE) and bar
  Kokhba's death.
- **Consequences**: catastrophic. Ancient sources report 580,000 killed in
  battle (probably exaggerated, but the casualties were enormous). Judea was
  depopulated and renamed **Syria Palaestina** (deliberately erasing the
  Jewish name). Jews were banned from Jerusalem (now "Aelia Capitolina").
- **Bar Kokhba's failure permanently discredited armed messianism** within
  mainstream Judaism. The rabbis turned away from both military resistance and
  specific messianic identifications. In the Talmud, his name was punned as
  "Bar Koziba" (son of the lie/disappointment).

## 5. The Samaritans

### Who They Were — and Are

The Samaritans are **not a Jewish sect** — they are a separate **Israelite
tradition** that claims independent descent from the ancient northern tribes
(primarily Ephraim and Manasseh). The split between Jews and Samaritans is
one of the oldest religious schisms in recorded history.

### The Origin Debate

Two competing narratives, each told by the opposing side:

| Jewish version (2 Kings 17) | Samaritan version |
|---|---|
| After the Assyrian conquest of the northern kingdom (722 BCE), the Assyrians deported Israelites and imported foreign peoples who mixed their religions with Israelite worship — producing a "syncretic" and illegitimate tradition. | The Samaritans are the true, unbroken Israelite tradition. **They** remained in the land while the southern kingdom (Judah) was corrupted during the Babylonian exile. The real schism occurred when Eli the priest wrongly moved the tabernacle from Mount Gerizim to Shiloh. |

**What modern scholarship says**: The truth is somewhere in between. Genetic
studies and archaeological evidence suggest the Samaritans have genuine
Israelite ancestry; the 2 Kings 17 account is polemical. But the Samaritan
tradition has also been shaped by centuries of separate development.

### Core Distinctive Beliefs

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Holy mountain** | **Mount Gerizim** (near Shechem/Nablus), not Jerusalem. The Samaritans built a temple there (probably 5th–4th century BCE); the Hasmonean ruler **John Hyrcanus I destroyed it c. 110 BCE** — the defining act of the rift. |
| **Scripture** | Only the **Pentateuch** (Torah, five books of Moses). They reject the Prophets, Writings, and all post-Mosaic texts. Their Pentateuch text differs from the Jewish Masoretic Text in ~6,000 places, including a commandment to worship on Mount Gerizim (added to the Tenth Commandment in the Samaritan version). |
| **No prophetic or rabbinic tradition** | No oral Torah, no Talmud, no prophetic canon. |
| **Messiah = "Taheb"** | The Samaritans expect a Moses-like restorer (the *Taheb*), not a Davidic king. |
| **Passover sacrifice** | The Samaritans still perform the **Passover lamb sacrifice** on Mount Gerizim to this day — the only community in the world that still does. |

### The Samaritans and the New Testament

- **The "Parable of the Good Samaritan"** (Luke 10:25–37): Only makes sense if
  you understand the depth of Jewish-Samaritan hostility. A Samaritan helping
  a (presumably Jewish) man was as shocking as a Catholic helping an Orangeman
  at the height of the Troubles.
- **The woman at the well** (John 4:7–26): Jesus' conversation crosses ethnic,
  religious, and gender boundaries. The woman's statement — "Our fathers
  worshiped on this mountain [Gerizim], but you say that in Jerusalem is the
  place where people ought to worship" — captures the core dispute.
- **Acts 8:4–25**: Early Christian mission to the Samaritans — one of the first
  steps outside "mainstream" Judaism.

### Today

The Samaritan community survives, barely:

- **Population**: approximately **900 people** (2024) — one of the smallest
  ethno-religious groups on earth.
- **Two communities**: about half live in **Holon** (near Tel Aviv, Israel) and
  half on **Mount Gerizim** (near Nablus, Palestinian territories).
- They hold Israeli citizenship and/or Palestinian Authority ID depending on
  location. They maintain neutrality in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
- **Genetic and demographic challenge**: Centuries of inbreeding in a tiny
  population have created serious genetic health risks. In recent decades,
  Samaritan men have married women from outside the community (primarily Jewish
  or Ukrainian women) who convert to the Samaritan religion.
- They continue to observe Passover with **animal sacrifice on Mount Gerizim**
  — an annual event that draws visitors and media coverage.

## 6. Apocalypticism — The Engine of Christianity

### What Apocalypticism Was

"Apocalypticism" is not a group but a **worldview** that cut across groups. It
is arguably the most important intellectual current in Second Temple Judaism
because it is the framework in which Christianity was born.

Core features:

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Dualism** | The world is divided between God and cosmic evil (Satan, Belial, the "sons of darkness"). This is not found in the earlier Hebrew Bible (Job's "Satan" is a member of God's court, not an enemy) — it develops under probable **Zoroastrian influence** during and after the Babylonian exile. |
| **Periodization of history** | History is divided into predetermined ages or periods, building toward a final climax. |
| **Imminent divine intervention** | God is about to act — *now*, in this generation — to overthrow evil, judge the wicked, and establish a new age of righteousness. |
| **Resurrection and judgment** | The dead will be raised for final judgment — the righteous to eternal life, the wicked to punishment. This idea enters Judaism only in the Second Temple period (Daniel 12:2 is the earliest clear reference, c. 165 BCE). |
| **Heavenly beings** | Elaborate hierarchies of angels and demons. Archangels (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael) fight cosmic battles against demonic forces. |
| **Revelation** | Divine secrets are revealed to a chosen seer — through visions, heavenly journeys, or angelic messengers. This is the root meaning of *apokalypsis* (Greek: "unveiling"). |

### Key Texts

| Text | Date | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Daniel 7–12** | c. 165 BCE (the only OT book scholars unanimously date to the Maccabean period) | The "Son of Man" coming on clouds; four beasts representing empires; resurrection of the dead (12:2) |
| **1 Enoch** (Book of Watchers, Parables, Astronomical Book) | 3rd–1st century BCE | Fallen angels (Watchers), cosmic judgment, the "Elect One" / "Son of Man" figure, heavenly journeys. Not in the Jewish or Protestant canon but in the Ethiopian canon. Quoted in Jude 14–15. |
| **Jubilees** | 2nd century BCE | Retelling of Genesis–Exodus with apocalyptic framework; angels vs. demons; calendar disputes. Found at Qumran. |
| **4 Ezra** (= 2 Esdras 3–14) | c. 100 CE | Theodicy after the Temple's destruction; visions of the messianic age and final judgment. |
| **2 Baruch** | c. 100 CE | Similar to 4 Ezra — responding to the Temple's destruction with apocalyptic hope. |
| **Dead Sea Scrolls** (War Scroll, Pesharim, etc.) | 2nd century BCE–1st century CE | Final battle between "Sons of Light" and "Sons of Darkness"; eschatological timetables. |

### Why This Matters for Understanding Jesus

**Jesus was an apocalypticist.** This is the majority scholarly position
(Schweitzer, Sanders, Ehrman, Allison, Fredriksen):

- He preached the **"kingdom of God"** — not a metaphor for personal spiritual
  growth, but an imminent cosmic event: God was about to intervene, overthrow
  evil, and establish his reign on earth.
- He predicted the Son of Man's arrival "before this generation passes away"
  (Mark 9:1, 13:30).
- He predicted the Temple's destruction (Mark 13:2) — whether before or after
  the event is debated.
- His ethics were **interim ethics** — radical demands (sell all you have, leave
  your family, turn the other cheek) that make sense as preparation for an
  imminent end, not as a sustainable social programme.
- Paul expected Jesus to return within his own lifetime (1 Thessalonians
  4:15–17: "We who are alive, who are left, shall be caught up together with
  them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air").

**The failure of this expectation** — Jesus did not return within a generation
— is one of the most important facts in the history of Christianity. The entire
subsequent development of Christian theology (the delay of the *parousia*,
Augustine's City of God, the shift from apocalyptic urgency to institutional
church) can be understood as an adaptation to this non-event.

## 7. Other Messianic Claimants — Jesus Was Not Unique

One of the most important pieces of context: **Jesus was one of many** would-be
messiahs and prophetic figures in this period. The list, all documented by
Josephus and/or other sources:

| Figure | Date | Claim / Activity | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Judas son of Hezekiah** | 4 BCE | Led a revolt in Galilee after Herod's death; raided the royal arsenal at Sepphoris | Crushed by Rome; Sepphoris burned |
| **Simon of Peraea** | 4 BCE | A slave of Herod who crowned himself king; burned the royal palace at Jericho | Killed by Roman troops |
| **Athronges** | 4 BCE | A shepherd who declared himself king and led guerrilla attacks on Romans and Herodians | Captured |
| **Judas of Galilee** | 6 CE | Led a tax revolt; founded the "Fourth Philosophy" (God alone is ruler) | Killed; his ideology persisted and helped trigger the revolt of 66 CE |
| **Theudas** | c. 44–46 CE | Led followers to the Jordan, promising to part the waters like Joshua | Beheaded by the Roman procurator Fadus (also mentioned in Acts 5:36) |
| **"The Egyptian"** | c. 56 CE | Led 30,000 (Josephus; probably exaggerated) to the Mount of Olives, promising that the walls of Jerusalem would collapse | Dispersed by Felix; the Egyptian escaped. Paul is mistaken for him in Acts 21:38. |
| **Various unnamed prophets** | 50s–60s CE | Josephus mentions multiple "imposters and deceivers" leading crowds into the wilderness with promises of divine signs | Killed or dispersed by Roman authorities |
| **Menahem ben Judah** | 66 CE | Grandson of Judas of Galilee; entered Jerusalem "like a king" during the revolt | Assassinated by rival Jewish faction |
| **Simon bar Giora** | 69–70 CE | Military leader of the revolt; entered the Temple in royal robes | Captured by Titus; displayed in triumph in Rome; executed |
| **Simon bar Kokhba** | 132–135 CE | Declared messiah by Rabbi Akiva; ruled an independent Jewish state for ~3 years | Killed at Betar; revolt crushed; Judaism permanently moved away from messianic activism |

### What This Tells Us

- The first century was **saturated with messianic expectation**. Jesus was part
  of a recognizable type — a charismatic leader claiming divine authorization
  in a context of Roman oppression and apocalyptic hope.
- **Every single one of these figures was killed or defeated.** None of them
  "came back." The Christian claim that Jesus was resurrected is what made the
  Jesus movement the exception — whether you believe the resurrection happened
  or interpret it as a psychological/visionary experience, the *belief* in
  resurrection is what separated this movement from the others.
- The existence of these other claimants **does not prove** Jesus was "just
  another one of them." But it does prove that his movement was not unique in
  kind — only in outcome.

## 8. Fact vs. Fiction Scorecard

### ESTABLISHED FACTS

1. First-century Judaism was not monolithic but comprised Pharisees, Sadducees,
   Essenes, Zealots/Sicarii, Samaritans, and various apocalyptic movements.
2. Josephus reports ~6,000 Pharisees and ~4,000 Essenes.
3. The Sadducees denied resurrection, oral Torah, and afterlife; they vanished
   after 70 CE.
4. ~900 Dead Sea Scrolls were found at Qumran (1947–1956), dating c. 250 BCE–
   68 CE.
5. The Samaritans are a separate Israelite tradition (not a Jewish sect) with
   ~900 members alive today.
6. Multiple messianic claimants appeared in the first century; all were killed
   or defeated.
7. Bar Kokhba was declared messiah by Rabbi Akiva and ruled an independent
   state c. 132–135 CE before being destroyed.
8. The destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE was the defining event that
   shaped both Rabbinic Judaism and Christianity.

### DEBATED BY SCHOLARS

1. Whether the Qumran community was Essene (majority says yes, but
   alternatives exist).
2. Whether the Masada mass suicide occurred as Josephus describes.
3. The exact nature of Pharisaic beliefs (our sources are hostile or
   retrospective).
4. The degree to which Jesus was "just another" apocalyptic prophet vs.
   categorically different.

### WHAT WE CANNOT ANSWER HISTORICALLY

1. Whether Jesus was "the" messiah in a theological sense — this is a
   faith claim, not a historical one.
2. Whether the resurrection of Jesus actually happened vs. being a visionary
   experience — historians can confirm the *belief* existed; they cannot
   confirm or deny the metaphysical claim.
