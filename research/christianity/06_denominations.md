# Major Denominations — How Christianity Fractured

## Overview

Christianity today comprises an estimated **45,000+ denominations** worldwide
(Center for the Study of Global Christianity). The major splits occurred in
three waves: the Oriental/Eastern split (5th c.), the East–West Schism (1054),
and the Protestant Reformation (16th c.).

---

## 1. Oriental Orthodox Churches (5th Century Split)

After the **Council of Chalcedon (451)**, churches that rejected the
"two natures" formula broke away:

- **Coptic Orthodox** (Egypt)
- **Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo**
- **Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo**
- **Syriac Orthodox**
- **Armenian Apostolic**
- **Malankara Orthodox** (India)

These churches affirm *miaphysitism* (one united nature of Christ, divine and
human) — which they distinguish from *monophysitism* (Christ has only a divine
nature). Modern ecumenical dialogues have acknowledged this was partly a
linguistic rather than substantive disagreement.

---

## 2. The East–West Schism (1054) — Catholic vs. Orthodox

| Issue | Roman Catholic position | Eastern Orthodox position |
|---|---|---|
| **Papal authority** | Pope has universal supremacy | Pope is "first among equals"; authority rests in councils |
| **Filioque** | Spirit proceeds from Father *and Son* | Spirit proceeds from Father alone (original creed) |
| **Ecclesiology** | Centralized under Rome | Autocephalous (self-governing) national churches |
| **Liturgy** | Latin rite (later vernacular post-Vatican II) | Divine Liturgy in local languages from the start |
| **Clergy** | Celibate priests (mandatory from 12th c.) | Married priests allowed; celibate bishops |
| **Purgatory** | Affirmed | Rejected as formal doctrine |

The schism was gradual — 1054 is a symbolic date (mutual excommunications
between Pope Leo IX's legate and Patriarch Michael Cerularius of
Constantinople). Full break solidified during the **Fourth Crusade (1204)**,
when Western Crusaders sacked Constantinople.

## 3. The Protestant Reformation (16th Century)

### Causes

- Corruption and financial abuses (sale of indulgences, simony, nepotism).
- Renaissance humanism — return to original texts (*ad fontes*).
- Printing press — enabled rapid spread of reformist ideas.
- Political factors — German princes saw opportunity to resist papal and
  imperial authority.

### Key Reformers and Movements

| Reformer / Movement | Location | Key ideas |
|---|---|---|
| **Martin Luther** (1517) | Germany | *Sola fide* (faith alone), *sola scriptura* (scripture alone); 95 Theses |
| **Huldrych Zwingli** (1520s) | Zurich | Radical reform of worship; symbolic Eucharist |
| **John Calvin** (1530s–40s) | Geneva | Predestination; theocratic governance; systematic theology (*Institutes*) |
| **Henry VIII** (1534) | England | Political break from Rome (Act of Supremacy); Church of England |
| **Anabaptists** (1520s) | Various | Believer's baptism (rejecting infant baptism); separation of church and state |
| **Counter-Reformation** | Catholic Church | Council of Trent (1545–1563); Jesuits; reformed abuses while reaffirming doctrine |

### Protestant Fragmentation

The principle of *sola scriptura* (the Bible as the sole authority) ironically
led to proliferation, since different readers reached different conclusions:

- **Lutherans** — follow Luther's theology.
- **Reformed/Presbyterian** — follow Calvin; emphasis on predestination.
- **Anglican/Episcopal** — "middle way" between Catholicism and Protestantism.
- **Baptist** — believer's baptism; congregational governance.
- **Methodist** — originated from John Wesley (18th c.); emphasis on holiness.
- **Pentecostal/Charismatic** — emphasis on gifts of the Spirit (tongues,
  healing); massive 20th-century growth.
- **Adventist, Restorationist, Non-denominational** — various later movements.

## 4. Key Theological Disagreements Across Denominations

| Doctrine | Catholic | Orthodox | Protestant (mainstream) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Authority** | Scripture + Tradition + Magisterium | Scripture + Tradition + Councils | Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) |
| **Salvation** | Faith + works + sacraments | Theosis (deification); synergy of grace and free will | Faith alone (*sola fide*) — varies by denomination |
| **Eucharist** | Transubstantiation (bread/wine become body/blood) | Divine mystery; real presence | Ranges from real presence (Lutheran) to symbolic (Baptist) |
| **Mary** | Immaculate Conception; Assumption; intercessor | Theotokos; ever-virgin; venerated but no Immaculate Conception dogma | Honoured but not venerated or prayed to |
| **Saints** | Canonised; intercession invoked | Venerated; intercession invoked | Respected as examples; no intercession |
| **Purgatory** | Affirmed | Rejected | Rejected |
| **Papal infallibility** | Affirmed (1870, Vatican I) | Rejected | Rejected |

## 5. Christianity by the Numbers (2025–2026 updates)

| Branch | Estimated adherents |
|---|---|
| Roman Catholic | ~1.27–1.38 billion |
| Protestant (all denominations) | ~900 million–1.13 billion |
| Eastern Orthodox | ~220 million |
| Oriental Orthodox | ~85 million |
| Other (Latter-day Saints, Jehovah's Witnesses, etc.) | ~40 million |
| **Total** | **~2.64 billion (~32.3% of world population)** |

### Recent Trends (Pew Research Center, Feb 2025)

- **Global growth**: Christianity is growing at **0.98% annually**, slightly
  faster than world population growth (0.88%). Growth is concentrated in
  sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and Latin America.
- **US plateau**: A major Pew Religious Landscape Study (2023–24 survey,
  published Feb 2025) found that the decades-long decline of US Christianity
  **appears to have leveled off**. As of 2025, ~62% of US adults identify as
  Christian (40% Protestant, 19% Catholic). The youngest adult cohort (born
  2000–2006) is **no less religious** than the cohort born in the 1990s.
- **Shifting patterns**: Decline is at the "high end" of belief and observance;
  growth is at the "low end" (least religious Americans); the "religious
  middle" is stable.
- **Spirituality trend**: Americans who identify with a religion are *more*
  likely than the religiously unaffiliated to say they have grown increasingly
  spiritual during their lives.
- **Global South growth**: The centre of gravity for Christianity continues to
  shift southward. Africa may have the world's largest Christian population by
  mid-century.
