# Councils, Creeds, and Canon — How "Orthodox" Christianity Was Built

## The Big Picture

The doctrines most Christians today consider essential — the Trinity, the full
divinity and humanity of Christ, the 27-book New Testament — were **not
settled in the first century**. They emerged through centuries of debate,
political manoeuvring, and council decisions.

---

## 1. Constantine and the Turning Point (312–325 CE)

- **312 CE**: Constantine wins the Battle of the Milvian Bridge; attributes
  victory to the Christian God.
- **313 CE**: Edict of Milan — Christianity becomes a legal religion
  (alongside others).
- **325 CE**: Constantine convenes the **Council of Nicaea** — the first
  empire-wide ("ecumenical") council.

> **Fact vs. fiction**: Constantine did **not** "make Christianity the state
> religion." That happened under Theodosius I in **380 CE** (Edict of
> Thessalonica). Constantine legalized it and favoured it, but paganism was
> not banned during his reign.

> Constantine was baptised only on his **deathbed** (337 CE). His motivations
> were a mix of personal conviction and political pragmatism.

---

## 2. The Major Ecumenical Councils

| Council | Year | Key issue | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nicaea I** | 325 | Was Jesus fully God or a created being? (Arius vs. Alexander/Athanasius) | **Nicene Creed**: Jesus is *homoousios* ("of the same substance") as the Father. Arianism condemned. |
| **Constantinople I** | 381 | Divinity of the Holy Spirit; revision of creed | Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed (the "Nicene Creed" recited today). Trinity formally defined. |
| **Ephesus** | 431 | Was Mary *Theotokos* ("God-bearer")? (Nestorius vs. Cyril) | Mary = Theotokos. Nestorianism condemned (Nestorian churches broke away). |
| **Chalcedon** | 451 | How are divinity and humanity united in Christ? | Christ has **two natures** (divine and human) in **one person**. Monophysites (one-nature) rejected this — leading to splits (Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac Orthodox churches). |
| **Constantinople II** | 553 | Clarification of Chalcedon | Condemned "Three Chapters" (writings deemed Nestorian). |
| **Constantinople III** | 680–681 | Did Christ have one will or two? | Two wills (divine and human). Monothelitism condemned. |
| **Nicaea II** | 787 | Veneration of icons | Icons are legitimate (*veneration*, not *worship*). Iconoclasm condemned. |

### What the Councils Reveal

- Orthodox doctrine was **not obvious or unanimous**. Each council was preceded
  by decades of fierce, often politically charged, debate.
- Losers at councils (Arians, Nestorians, Monophysites) were not "obviously
  wrong" — they read the same scriptures and had sophisticated theologies.
- Imperial politics played a decisive role. Emperors convened councils, enforced
  decisions, and sometimes reversed them.

## 3. The Trinity — Development, Not Revelation

The word "Trinity" does not appear in the Bible. The concept developed gradually:

1. **New Testament**: No explicit Trinitarian formula. Matthew 28:19 ("baptising
   in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit") is the closest, but many
   scholars consider it a later liturgical addition. Paul speaks of God, Christ,
   and the Spirit but never defines their metaphysical relationship.

2. **2nd century**: Early "Logos theology" (Justin Martyr, c. 150) —
   Christ as the divine Word/Reason, distinct from the Father.

3. **3rd century**: Tertullian (c. 200) coins *trinitas* in Latin; Origen
   develops a subordinationist model (the Son is subordinate to the Father).

4. **4th century**: The Arian controversy forces precision. Nicaea (325) and
   Constantinople (381) settle on *homoousios* — three persons, one substance.

5. **5th century onward**: Augustine's *De Trinitate* shapes Western
   Trinitarian theology.

> **Fact**: The Trinity is a doctrinal development, not a direct teaching of
> Jesus or the earliest Christians. This is acknowledged by mainstream
> theologians and historians alike.

## 4. The Biblical Canon — A Human Process

(See also [03_early_texts.md](03_early_texts.md))

Key milestones:
- **Marcion** (c. 140 CE) — first known attempt to define a Christian canon
  (Luke + 10 Pauline letters, rejecting the Old Testament). Deemed heretical.
- **Muratorian Fragment** (c. 170–200 CE) — earliest known orthodox canon list;
  includes most but not all of the current 27 books.
- **Eusebius** (c. 313 CE) — categorises books as "recognised," "disputed," and
  "spurious."
- **Athanasius' Easter Letter** (367 CE) — first exact match to the modern 27-
  book canon.
- **Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397)** — ratified the list.

Books on the fence for centuries: Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, 2–3 John, Jude,
Revelation. The Shepherd of Hermas and the Didache were used as scripture in
some churches for centuries.

> **Fact**: The canon was not "handed down complete." It was a gradual
> consensus shaped by theology, politics, and usage.

---

## 5. The Filioque Controversy and the Great Schism (1054 CE)

- The Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed (381) states the Holy Spirit "proceeds
  from the Father."
- The Western (Latin) church added *filioque* ("and the Son") without
  Eastern consent — first locally (6th century Spain), then officially adopted
  by Rome.
- The Eastern churches considered this an unauthorised alteration of a
  conciliar creed — theologically and procedurally.
- Combined with disputes over papal authority, this led to the **Great Schism
  of 1054** between Roman Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy.
